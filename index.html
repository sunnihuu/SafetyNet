SafetyNet

NYC already has an emergency network. It lives in the bodega, the halal cart, the doorman. SafetyNet makes it official.

01 Context

When centralized systems fail, people turn to who they trust.

NYC's emergency communication runs on centralized infrastructure: alerts, apps, broadcasts. These systems assume stable signal, consistent digital access, and a shared language. In a real crisis, those assumptions rarely hold.

An informal system already exists and quietly functions. Bodegas stay open through storms. Halal cart operators know their block. Doormen have residents' trust built over years. New Yorkers know who to turn to. They just don't have a way to find them when it matters most.

"There is information, but it's not reliable or clear enough to act on."

Patrick, Queens resident, interviewed during research
The unofficial anchor of NYC neighborhood life
02 Key Insight

"I love my customers. I want to protect them, and I appreciate being someone they can turn to during danger."

Kareem, Owner, Hooda Halal, W 115th St & Broadway

The informal trust network already exists across every NYC neighborhood. Local vendors, shop owners, and building staff naturally step into supportive roles during emergencies, across languages, backgrounds, and income levels. The gap isn't motivation. It's structure, visibility, and connection to official information.

03 Users & Stakeholders

Who this is for, and who makes it work.

Primary Users

Residents & Newcomers

Students, tourists, and recent arrivals who lack local knowledge and trusted networks during emergencies. A survey of 12 newcomers found consistent uncertainty about where to go and who to trust in a crisis.

Primary

Hub Operators

Bodegas, Food Carts & Doormen

Community-trusted individuals who already serve as informal safety points across languages and backgrounds. They often stay open during disasters, motivated by neighborhood relationships. SafetyNet offers them recognition, permit benefits, and direct city connection.

Stakeholder

Institutional Partners

City & Agencies

NYC Mayor's Office, NYC Emergency Management (NYCEM), and First Responders. The city gains a scalable, decentralized communication layer and real-time ground-level intelligence, without building any new infrastructure from scratch.

Partner
04 Solution

A distributed network of everyday trust.

SafetyNet formalizes what already exists: a citywide network of vetted local establishments (bodegas, halal carts, coffee shops, doormen) that act as verified emergency communication hubs. Marked with visible signage, mapped digitally, and connected to city information systems.

01

Find

Locate the nearest SafetyNet hub through the app map, a Google Maps layer, or a physical sticker on the storefront. No stable signal needed to recognize it in person.

02

Access

Receive emergency information packets and verbal guidance from trusted vendors. In your language, from a familiar face, with backup communication when digital channels fail.

03

Feedback

Hub operators share real-time local conditions: blocked streets, supply gaps, unmet needs. This ground-level intelligence flows back into city systems for faster, more accurate response.

05 How It Works

A three-way information flow.

City → Hubs → Residents

Top Down

NYC Emergency Management sends verified, neighborhood-specific alerts to hub operators. Hubs distribute city-issued emergency packets and provide on-the-ground guidance to residents who come to them.

Before a disaster: NYCEM supplies printed protocols, resource packets, and basic supplies to every vetted hub.

Residents → Hubs → City

Bottom Up

Hub operators observe local conditions and report unmet needs back to the city through the app or paper logs: supply shortages, blocked routes, populations that haven't been reached.

After a disaster: Hub reports help identify underserved areas and improve future emergency planning.

Physical + Digital

Redundant

The system works online through the app and offline through physical signs and pre-stocked emergency packets. Satellite phones enable hub reporting even when all cell towers are down.

Designed around iOS 16.1+ satellite messaging and paper log backups when every digital channel fails.

07 System Ecosystem

Before and After SafetyNet.

Before

Fragmented, invisible system

  • Informal trust networks exist but are unstructured and invisible
  • Newcomers don't know who to turn to or where to go
  • Official messaging misses communities with language barriers
  • The city has no ground-level feedback during active disasters
  • Hubs operate in isolation with no connection to each other or to city systems
After

Connected, distributed network

  • Vetted hubs are visible on the map and in person through storefront signage
  • Newcomers can find trusted help within 0.2 miles
  • Multi-language guidance from familiar neighborhood faces
  • Real-time bottom-up intelligence feeds city emergency systems
  • City, hubs, and residents in continuous two-way communication

10 Fairness & Accountability

How the system delivers equitably.

Access

Hubs are findable through the app map and through physical storefront signs. No stable signal, battery, or app required to reach help.

Both digital and physical pathways, by design

Language and Culture

Hubs reflect the languages and cultures of their neighborhoods. Guidance arrives in familiar, everyday language from a known face, serving newcomers, immigrants, and non-English speakers.

Culturally familiar communication, without translation barriers

Distribution

Hubs are identified neighborhood by neighborhood. Each community nominates its own trusted places. No single central solution is imposed on all.

Neighborhood-nominated, not top-down assigned

Accountability

Hubs are vetted, trained, and connected to city systems, with incentives and periodic check-ins. The system prevents purely informal or unregulated networks from forming.

City-integrated, not left to informal coordination
11 Reflection

What we learned. What comes next.

SafetyNet began with a simple observation: during a crisis, New Yorkers don't turn to apps. They turn to people. The design didn't invent new behavior. It gave existing behavior a structure it deserved.

1

Formalize existing behavior

The most durable systems work with human instinct, not against it. The bodega was already a safety hub. We just made it visible and connected it to city resources.

2

Design for the edges of connectivity

Offline-first thinking forced us to prioritize physical touchpoints. The storefront sign became as important as the app, and more resilient in a real emergency.

3

Trust is hyperlocal

A national platform can't replicate the trust built between Kareem and his regulars over years. Scalability means replicating the structure, not the relationships.

4

Next steps

A pilot in one NYC neighborhood, recruiting hubs, testing signage, and running a tabletop emergency drill, would validate the model before any city-scale rollout.
